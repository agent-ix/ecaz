# Review: Packet 274 — C1 ADR-029 Rank Correlation Study

Reviewer: agent-ix (via code-review)
Date: 2026-04-12

## Assessment: Strong positive result — ADR-029 passes its first gate convincingly

### What this packet did right

1. **Ran both synthetic and real-corpus studies.** The synthetic clustered corpus
   established the baseline, and the real `tqhnsw_real_10k` export confirmed that real
   data is even more favorable. This is the right validation order.

2. **The correlation numbers are excellent.** Spearman ρ = 0.9999–1.0000 across both
   corpora far exceeds ADR-029's ρ ≥ 0.9 gate. The int8 quantization preserves rank
   ordering almost perfectly for this codebook size and data distribution.

3. **The survivor capture metric is the key finding.** `exact_top10_captured_by_approx_top20
   = 1.0000` on all 20 real queries means a 2x survivor budget (keep top-20 approximate,
   exact-rescore to find top-10) loses zero true neighbors. This is the number that
   justifies the two-stage pipeline.

4. **The study binary is well-designed.** Supporting both synthetic and file-based corpus
   input, reporting correlation + overlap + capture + timing in one run, and keeping this
   out of scan execution are all correct choices for a research tool.

### The 1.7x speedup is underwhelming — but expected for scalar int8

The scalar int8 loop (`prod.rs:319`) has the same structure as the scalar f32 loop: nibble
extract → table lookup → multiply → accumulate. The only difference is i8 × i8 → i32 vs
f32 × f32 → f32. On modern x86, integer multiply throughput is comparable to float, so
1.7x is reasonable for scalar.

The real payoff comes from SIMD. The int8 representation enables `vpshufb` for the codebook
lookup (32 lookups per cycle) and `vpmaddubsw` / `vpmaddwd` for the accumulation (16-32
multiply-adds per cycle). The scalar 1.7x is the floor, not the ceiling.

**This is the most important takeaway: the study validates that int8 quantization preserves
rank ordering, which is the prerequisite for SIMD. The actual speedup will come from a
vectorized implementation, not from the scalar int8 path tested here.**

### The approximate scorer implementation is correct

Looking at `prod.rs:215-241` and `prod.rs:319-353`:

- `prepare_ip_query_int8_approx_no_qjl_4bit` quantizes both the codebook and the rotated
  query to i8, preserving the product scale factor for reconstruction
- `score_ip_from_split_parts_int8_approx_no_qjl_4bit` accumulates in i32 (no overflow
  risk for 1536 dims with i8 × i8 products: max |sum| = 1536 × 127 × 127 ≈ 24.8M, well
  within i32 range)
- The lane guard (`self.bits == 4 && !qjl_enabled(...)`) prevents misuse on other paths
- The `score_scale` factor correctly reconstructs the approximate f32 score from the i32
  accumulator

### Questions the study doesn't yet answer

1. **Beam-search candidate traces.** The study scores all 10K vectors against each query.
   In real beam search, only ~300-400 candidates are scored per query, and they're biased
   toward the neighborhood of the query. The correlation might differ on this subset (likely
   better, since nearby candidates are more similar and harder to distinguish — but the
   study doesn't confirm this).

2. **Per-expansion survival rate.** The study measures global top-k capture. In beam search,
   the filter runs per-source-expansion (at most `2m = 16` candidates per source). The
   effective rejection rate and capture quality at expansion granularity may differ from the
   global metrics.

3. **SIMD implementation feasibility.** The study validates the int8 representation but
   the actual vpshufb/vpmaddubsw loop hasn't been prototyped. The implementation may face
   challenges with the nibble extraction + per-lane vpshufb addressing (the 256-bit vpshufb
   operates independently on two 128-bit lanes).

## Suggestions

1. **Merge the study infrastructure.** The binary and the int8 scorer are useful regardless
   of whether the runtime integration succeeds.

2. **The next step is the SIMD int8 scorer, not more correlation studies.** The rank
   correlation is so strong (ρ > 0.999) that further corpus-level validation adds nothing.
   The bottleneck is now implementation: can a vpshufb-based int8 loop deliver the 10-50x
   speedup that justifies the two-stage pipeline?

3. **When prototyping the SIMD scorer, benchmark it standalone before wiring into beam
   search.** Packet 275 shows that the runtime integration overhead can eat the scoring
   savings. Get the per-score microbenchmark right first.
