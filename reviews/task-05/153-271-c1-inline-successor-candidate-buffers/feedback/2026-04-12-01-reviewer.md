# Review: Packet 271 — C1 Inline Successor Candidate Buffers

Reviewer: agent-ix (via code-review)
Date: 2026-04-12

## Assessment: Correct discard — the hypothesis was reasonable but the measurement was clear

### What this packet did right

1. **Tested a natural follow-up to packet 270.** If removing the neighbor-tid Vec helped,
   replacing the successor-candidate Vec with `SmallVec<[BeamCandidate; 16]>` was the
   obvious next experiment. Running it and measuring was the right call.

2. **Discarded cleanly when the numbers didn't move.** Two valid reruns showed flat-to-
   slightly-worse results. The packet didn't try to rescue the change by adding more
   complexity or cherry-picking a favorable run.

3. **The SmallVec dependency was not landed.** Reverting instead of keeping dead complexity
   is correct.

### Why it didn't help

The successor `Vec<BeamCandidate>` is capped at `2m = 16` entries. Each `BeamCandidate` is
small (a TID + f32 score + metadata). At 16 entries, the allocator probably serves this from
a thread-local small-size bin in ~20-30ns. SmallVec avoids that allocation but adds its own
branch-on-spill overhead. At 16 entries the two costs are comparable — the allocator is fast
for small, short-lived vectors.

This suggests that the remaining allocation overhead is not in the successor buffers but in
the **element load path**: the `read_page_tuple_bytes` → `.to_vec()` → `decode` → `Arc::new()`
chain where each call allocates 800+ bytes, not 16-entry structs.

### The negative `clock_timestamp()` observation

The invalid first run (`min=-799.355ms`) on WSL2 is concerning. Packet 273 addresses this,
but it's worth noting that WSL2's `clock_timestamp()` instability affects the reliability of
small deltas. The fact that this packet saw flat results across two valid runs, not just one,
strengthens the discard decision.

## Suggestions

1. **Record and move on.** The discard is correct.

2. **Skip further container-type experiments.** SmallVec, ArrayVec, and similar won't move
   the needle when the allocator is already fast for small counts. The remaining allocation
   cost is in the graph decode path (large byte copies), not in small struct vectors.
