# Feedback: C1 ADR-032 Node-Centric Redesign Reframe

**Reviewer:** Claude (Opus 4.6)
**Date:** 2026-04-13

## Verdict: The exact-on-head frontier promotion cut is a significant result

This is a dense packet — three rejected cuts and one breakthrough. The
methodical escalation from "swap containers" through "fuse caches" through
"slot-based frontier" to "change the exact-score lifecycle" is exactly the
right investigative discipline. Each rejected cut narrowed the hypothesis
space until the actual winning lever was found.

## Findings on the rejected cuts

### First cut (fused element-local neighbor/score state): correctly discarded

Still operating on tids, still doing the same work per step, just with a
wider object per cache entry. The ~80-90us regression confirms that
interior mutability overhead (lazy neighbors behind `Option`, exact-score
state as enum) costs more than the join-savings when traversal still keys
everything by tid.

### Second cut (slot-based frontier + bootstrap scheduler): informative failure

This is the first cut that exercised the actual ADR-032 architecture.
The ~70-80us regression is smaller than the fused-node cut, which is
encouraging — slots are slightly better than tids — but it confirms that
merely changing the addressing scheme is not the win. The work per step
is unchanged.

### Score-pressure diagnostic: critical evidence

The score-pressure readout is the most important analytical step in this
packet. Key observations:

- `candidate_score_calls` barely changes between ADR-031 on/off (~521 vs
  ~527). This means the binary prefilter is not cutting much exact-score
  volume.
- `graph_element_cache_misses` is identical (~527.5) in both cases. The
  cache miss rate is dominated by graph structure, not by prefilter state.
- `rescan_layer0_seed_elapsed_us` is ~280us *higher* with ADR-031 enabled
  (1495 vs 1216). That overhead comes from the binary scoring path itself.

This correctly led to the insight that the next lever is **when** exact
scoring happens, not **how many** candidates are scored.

## Findings on the exact-on-head frontier promotion cut

### The latency result is striking

| ef_search | ADR-031 T1 mean | ADR-032 mean | speedup |
|-----------|----------------|--------------|---------|
| 40        | ~1.51ms        | ~0.90ms      | ~1.68x  |
| 128       | ~3.41ms        | ~1.66ms      | ~2.05x  |
| 200       | ~4.77ms        | ~2.38ms      | ~2.00x  |

A consistent ~2x speedup at higher ef is not an incremental improvement.
It's a qualitative change in the latency/ef-search curve.

### The recall trade-off at ef=40 is the critical concern

At `ef_search=40`:
- ADR-031 T1 recall is not reported here but the standing baseline was
  higher
- ADR-032 exact-on-head: `graph_recall_at_10 = 0.8080`

At `ef_search=128`: `graph_recall_at_10 = 0.8861` with
`graph_below_exact_queries = 12`, `worst_exact_gap = 1`.

The recall at `ef=40` is equal to `exact_quantized_recall_at_10 = 0.8080`,
which is the theoretical floor — the scan is returning exactly the set that
quantized exact scoring would find, no better. This means the frontier
promotion is not doing enough exploration at low ef to find candidates that
the approximate score would miss.

At higher ef, the additional exploration budget overcomes this and recall
climbs above the quantized floor, which is the expected behavior.

### Code review: the promotion loop

The `consume_candidate_frontier_head_with_exact_promotion` function at
scan.rs:1793-1829 implements a clean loop:

1. Pop frontier head (approximate-scored candidate)
2. Exact-score it
3. Compare exact score against next frontier head's approximate score
4. If still best, return it; otherwise requeue with exact score and retry

This is correct but has a subtle property: the comparison at line 1819 is
`exact_candidate.score <= next` where `next` is an *approximate* score.
This means a candidate that exact-scores to slightly worse than the next
approximate candidate gets requeued, even if that next candidate would
exact-score to something even worse. In the worst case this could cause a
cascade of requeue-then-repop cycles.

**Question for coder-1:** Have you measured the average number of requeue
iterations per frontier consumption? If this is consistently 1-2, the loop
is fine. If it's occasionally high (>5), it could become a latency tail
risk at higher ef values.

### Code review: the loaded state simplification

The diff removes `LoadedElementScoreInput` and the `ExactPayload` variant
entirely. Binary-prefiltered candidates now enter the frontier with only
`approx_score` and `node` tid — no pre-loaded gamma/code_bytes.

This is clean. The pre-loading was an optimization for eager exact scoring;
since exact scoring is now deferred to head-promotion time, carrying the
payload through the frontier is wasted work. The exact-score path in the
promotion loop calls `cached_graph_element_and_score` which re-fetches
from the element cache — and since the element was already cached during
the binary prefilter pass, this is a cache hit.

### `graph_below_exact_queries` at higher ef

At `ef=128`: 12 queries out of 1000 have `graph_below_exact_queries > 0`.
At `ef=200`: 13 queries. The `worst_exact_gap = 1` means at most one
result position is worse than exact-quantized would produce.

This is acceptable for a first cut but worth watching. If the promotion
timing is systematically under-exploring certain graph neighborhoods, this
gap could widen on different data distributions.

## Investigative paths forward

### Path A: Hybrid promotion with ef-adaptive eagerness

The recall loss at low ef suggests that deferred exact scoring needs a
minimum exploration guarantee. One approach:

- At low ef (e.g., ef <= 64), exact-score the top-k binary survivors
  eagerly during source expansion (current ADR-031 behavior)
- At high ef (e.g., ef > 64), use the deferred head-promotion path

This gives the best of both worlds: ADR-031 recall at low ef, ADR-032
speed at high ef. The threshold could be derived from the frontier
capacity or from the survivor budget ratio.

### Path B: Promotion with lookahead depth

Instead of comparing exact score against only the next frontier head,
compare against the approximate scores of the top-k frontier entries (e.g.,
k=3). This would reduce unnecessary requeue cascades and give the
promotion decision more stability without adding much cost (peeking at a
sorted frontier is cheap).

### Path C: Approximate-score recalibration

The approximate scores from the binary sign-word scorer have a different
scale and noise profile than exact scores. The current comparison at
line 1819 treats them as directly comparable. A calibration factor (even a
simple constant multiplier derived from the score-pressure diagnostic)
could make the promotion decision more accurate and reduce both requeue
churn and recall loss.

### Path D: Score-budget accounting

Track per-scan exact-score budget consumption. If the scan is completing
with significant unused budget (i.e., it's scoring far fewer candidates
than the ADR-031 path would), there's room to add opportunistic exact
scoring during expansion for the most promising candidates. This would be
a principled version of Path A, driven by actual budget rather than a fixed
ef threshold.

### Path E: Keep pushing on the current cut

Before any of the above, the simplest next step is to measure the kept
ADR-031 recall at `ef=40` on the same 1000-query corpus. If ADR-031 T1
at `ef=40` was also near the 0.8080 quantized floor, then the recall
"regression" is not a regression at all — it's parity. That would make
this cut a clean keep candidate without modifications.

## Summary

The investigation arc on this packet is exemplary. Three principled
failures that each narrowed the hypothesis, followed by a diagnostic that
identified the right lever, followed by a cut that delivers a ~2x latency
improvement at higher ef. The remaining question is whether the recall
trade-off at low ef is acceptable or needs a hybrid approach.

Recommendation: measure ADR-031 T1 recall at `ef=40` on the same corpus
(Path E) before adding complexity. If recall is comparable, this cut is
close to a keep as-is.
