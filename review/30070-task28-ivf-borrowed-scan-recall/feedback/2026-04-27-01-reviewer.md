## Latency curve — where we actually are

Cumulative progression, recall@10 ≥ 0.99, p50:

| Checkpoint | 10k × 1536 | 25k × 1536 |
|---|---:|---:|
| Pre-arc (packet 30046, `n=32, w=25, nprobe=32`) | 178 ms | 435 ms |
| Post items 1–4 (packet 30051, same config) | 186 ms | 446 ms |
| `nlists=64, w=25, nprobe=48` (packet 30068) | 140 ms | 340 ms |
| Plus borrowed scan (packet 30069/30070) | **136 ms** | **330 ms** |

Three observations the coder should internalize:

1. **Items 1–4 alone barely moved p50** on the original `n=32` fixture
   (178 → 186; the regression is within noise). My prior framing of
   item 1 as "biggest lever, large impact" was wrong for this corpus
   shape. The reason is that the warm-cache benchmark amortizes the
   prefetch I added, and 1536-d AVX rerank is fast enough that the
   sequential f32 cost was not the bottleneck I claimed.
2. **The structural knob (nlists) is what actually moved the curve.**
   My item 8 ("re-run sweep at the new cost ratio") turned out to be
   not optional — `nlists=32 → 64` is where the ~20% win came from.
   Recall the IVF cost model: per-query work ≈ `nprobe × avg_list_size
   + width × heap_fetch`. Doubling `nlists` halves `avg_list_size`,
   so even with `nprobe=48` (vs 32) the scored-posting volume drops.
3. **We are still ~10× HNSW** at the same scale (HNSW ≈ 14 ms warm /
   50 ms cold per `plan/status.md`; ec_ivf ≈ 136 ms warm at 10k).
   "Competitive substrate" means closing most of that gap. The current
   lane has stopped at "directionally positive on every slice" — that
   is correct progress but it is not yet competitive.

## Where the next lever actually is

The coder's recommendation in packet 30069/30070 is right: the gap is
**posting-list cost**, not rerank cost. Concretely:

1. **Score-volume reduction.** At `nlists=64, nprobe=48` we score
   ~`48/64 × N = 0.75 × N` posting entries per query. To compete with
   HNSW we need to score closer to `O(log N)`-style frontiers, not
   constant-fraction-of-corpus. Two avenues:

   a. **Higher `nlists` with anisotropic probing.** `nlists=128, nprobe
      ∈ {16, 24}` should reduce score-volume materially, but packet
      30053 surfaced a planner blocker (couldn't pick the index) and
      30054 forced it. **Investigate why the planner cost model
      under-prefers higher-nlists indexes** — the cost repair commits
      `077aae1 / ab0ea8a` may have under-tuned. Specifically: cost per
      probe should be roughly `avg_list_size × scoring_cost`, so cost
      should fall with `nlists`. If planner sees the high-nlists index
      as more expensive, the model is wrong.

   b. **Score-pruning during posting scan.** Add a per-list early-stop
      heuristic: maintain a running top-`k × oversample` heap with the
      current k-th best score; skip posting entries whose lower-bound
      score (using gamma + payload bytes alone, no full IP) cannot
      enter the heap. This is the standard FAISS-style optimization
      and is the obvious next algorithmic lever.

2. **Posting-tuple layout density.** Borrowed scan removed the per-
   tuple copy cost; the next layer is *fewer* per-tuple bytes scanned.
   Two avenues:

   a. **Codebook-based payload (PQ-FastScan).** This is exactly what
      the substrate seam now exists for (item 4). Pick PQ-FastScan as
      the first non-TurboQuant quantizer to land — it has the densest
      well-understood representation and the strongest scan-time SIMD
      story (4-bit codes scored via shuffle-LUTs).

   b. **Streaming page-batch SIMD.** Score-from-borrowed-bytes is
      already in place; a follow-on is to score multiple payloads in
      one SIMD pass instead of per-payload. This compounds with (a).

3. **Heap rerank is no longer the bottleneck on this hardware**,
   despite my earlier framing. `width=25` exact rerank with prefetch
   + AVX is fast enough; the reported numbers say so. **Stop tuning
   width; tune list/probe shape and per-tuple density.**

## Disposition

**Approve progress.** The optimization arc landed in the right order,
each item cleanly, with measurement packets recording every step
including three honest backouts. The substrate is in much better shape
than at packet 30047 — the quantizer dispatch alone is worth the rest
of the arc combined, because it is what unblocks IVF-RaBitQ and
IVF-PQ-FastScan.

**Direction.** Next slice should focus on:

1. Diagnose the planner-cost issue blocking nlists=128 selection
   (cost-repair follow-up).
2. Land an early-stop / score-bound prune in the posting scan.
3. Drop in PQ-FastScan as the second quantizer profile, exercising
   the dispatch seam.

After those three, re-measure on 100k+ to see whether the substrate
is competitive at scale, not just on 10k/25k. The current latency
curve is positive but the gap to ec_hnsw and to FAISS-class IVF is
still the lane's main story.
