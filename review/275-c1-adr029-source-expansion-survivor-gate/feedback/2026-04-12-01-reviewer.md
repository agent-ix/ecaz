# Review: Packet 275 — C1 ADR-029 Source Expansion Survivor Gate

Reviewer: agent-ix (via code-review)
Date: 2026-04-12

## Assessment: Correct discard — the integration point was wrong, not the idea

### What this packet did right

1. **Ran the experiment on real corpus, not synthetic.** The verified warm real-corpus
   surface is the right gate for runtime changes.

2. **Tested two budget levels (12 and 8).** Both regressed. This rules out the possibility
   that the budget was simply too conservative — even at budget=8 (keeping 50% of 2m=16
   candidates), the overhead exceeded the savings.

3. **Discarded cleanly without distorting the interpretation.** The packet correctly
   identifies the shape mismatch: per-source adjacency is too small for a two-stage
   filter to pay off.

### Why the source-expansion seam doesn't work

The math is clear: with `m=8`, each source expansion produces at most `2m = 16` neighbor
candidates. The approximate scorer at 1.7x faster (scalar) saves ~400ns per score. Over
16 candidates, that's ~6.4μs saved. But the two-stage pipeline adds:

- Approximate query preparation overhead (one-time per query, amortized — small)
- Per-expansion: approximate-score all 16 → sort/select survivors → exact-score survivors
- The sort/select step for 16 elements costs ~200-400ns
- The approximate scoring of 16 elements at ~800ns each costs ~12.8μs

So the approximate pass costs ~13μs and saves at most ~6.4μs (if half the candidates are
rejected). Net result: **slower**, exactly as measured.

### The right integration point is higher up

The two-stage filter makes sense when the candidate pool is large enough that the savings
from rejecting candidates exceed the fixed cost of the approximate pass. At 16 candidates
per source, the pool is too small.

Better integration points:

1. **Batch pre-filter across multiple expansions.** Accumulate uncached neighbor TIDs from
   several source expansions, approximate-score the whole batch, then exact-score only
   survivors. This amortizes the sort/select overhead over a larger pool.

2. **Beam-level pre-filter.** Before entering a new expansion, approximate-score all
   current beam candidates to decide which sources are worth expanding at all. This is a
   different architectural pattern (pre-filter on expansion priority, not on successor
   scoring).

3. **SIMD scorer first.** The 1.7x scalar speedup means the approximate scorer is only
   marginally cheaper than the exact scorer. At 10-50x (with vpshufb), the per-candidate
   savings become large enough that even a 16-candidate pool is worth filtering. **The
   source-expansion seam might work fine once the approximate scorer is actually fast.**

### Implication for ADR-029

This discard does **not** invalidate ADR-029. It validates the research direction (packet
274's correlation data is strong) but shows that the scalar int8 approximation isn't cheap
enough relative to the exact scorer to justify the two-stage overhead at per-source
granularity.

The path forward is:
1. Make the approximate scorer 10-50x faster (SIMD), not 1.7x
2. Then re-test the source-expansion seam — it may work at that speedup ratio
3. Or find a higher-level integration point where the candidate pool is larger

## Suggestions

1. **Record and move on.** The discard is correct.

2. **Do not attempt another runtime integration until the SIMD int8 scorer exists.** The
   1.7x scalar speedup is insufficient for any integration point. The overhead of the
   two-stage pipeline requires at least ~5x per-score improvement to break even at
   expansion granularity.

3. **When the SIMD scorer is ready, revisit this exact seam first.** The source-expansion
   point in `cached_scan_successor_candidates_for_layer` is the simplest integration. If
   the SIMD scorer makes it work, there's no need for the more complex batch or beam-level
   approaches.
