# Feedback: C1 ADR-032 Neighbor-Cache Arena Slice

**Reviewer:** Claude (Opus 4.6)
**Date:** 2026-04-13

## Verdict: Discard acknowledged, stronger regression than 291

The neighbor-cache arena was a reasonable next step after the element-cache
arena, and the inline `CachedNeighborTids::{Inline, Heap}` fast path for
the common `2m=16` adjacency width was a sound idea. But the regression is
larger than packet 291 (~130-135us mean regression vs ~30-35us), and it's
consistent.

## Findings

### 1. The inline fast path is good engineering but irrelevant here

The `Inline` variant avoiding a `Vec<ItemPointer>` allocation for small
adjacency lists is the kind of optimization that matters at scale or under
allocation pressure. But on the warm path, the neighbor cache has an
extremely high hit rate (~48.5 misses per query). Almost all neighbor
accesses are served from cache. The `Vec` allocation only happens on miss.

At ~48.5 misses per query, saving a `Vec` allocation per miss saves maybe
~1us total. The arena indirection and map reshaping costs more than that.

### 2. The regression is larger than 291, which is informative

Packet 291 (element-only arena): ~30-35us regression.
Packet 292 (neighbor-only arena): ~130-135us regression.

The neighbor cache path is more sensitive to access-pattern changes because
neighbor lookups sit inside the inner expansion loop — each source
expansion fans out to `2m` neighbor lookups. Adding any per-access overhead
here multiplies by the fan-out factor.

### 3. Flaky `pgrx test` observation

The note about needing one rerun due to order-sensitive frontier-shape pg
tests failing intermittently is worth flagging. If this is a known flake
unrelated to the change, it should be tracked. If it's new, it could
indicate that the arena reshaping changed iteration order in a way that
exposed a latent test sensitivity. Since the slice was discarded, this is
moot here, but it's worth monitoring.

## Conclusion

This packet, combined with 291, provides clear evidence that isolated
container-shape substitutions inside the scan caches are not a productive
optimization lane. The discard decision and the explicit recommendation to
pursue a larger-scope change are both correct.
